Strengthening Research Security in Canada: A Risk-Based Framework with Digital Tools

View on GitHub

The Growing Importance of Research Security in Canada

Canada's world-class research institutions are at the forefront of innovation, attracting global collaborations and funding. However, with increasing geopolitical tensions and sophisticated cyber threats, safeguarding Canadian research from foreign interference, intellectual property theft, and unintended technology transfers has become a national priority.

A Research Security Risk Assessment Framework helps Canadian universities, government labs, and private-sector R&D centers:

  • Protect sensitive research in critical technologies (AI, quantum, biotech, defense)
  • Comply with federal guidelines, including the National Security Guidelines for Research Partnerships (NSGRP) and Safeguarding Your Research policies
  • Mitigate risks while maintaining Canada's tradition of open, collaborative science

Designing a Canadian Research Security Framework

1. Identifying Key Threats

Canadian research faces unique risks, including:

  • Foreign state-sponsored interference in academic collaborations
  • Cyber espionage targeting intellectual property
  • Unintended technology transfers to high-risk entities
  • Export control violations (e.g., sharing restricted tech with sanctioned entities)

2. Assessing Vulnerabilities

A risk-based approach evaluates:

  • Research sensitivity (dual-use, military applications)
  • Funding sources (foreign vs. domestic, industry vs. government)
  • Collaboration risks (partners in high-risk jurisdictions)
  • Data security (controlled, classified, or proprietary information)

3. Tailoring to Canadian Research Priorities

  • AI & Advanced Computing: Preventing misuse in surveillance or autonomous weapons
  • Quantum Technologies: Protecting cryptographic and sensing breakthroughs
  • Biotechnology & Health Research: Securing genomic data and bio-manufacturing IP
  • Energy & Cleantech: Safeguarding critical infrastructure research

Implementing Research Security in Canadian Institutions

1. Integrating Security into the Research Lifecycle

  • Pre-Award Screening: Assessing risks before grants are approved (aligned with NSGRP)
  • Collaborator Due Diligence: Vetting international partners against sanctions lists
  • Ongoing Monitoring: Tracking changes in project risk profiles
  • Publication & Tech Transfer Reviews: Preventing unintended disclosures

2. Cross-Institutional Collaboration

  • University Research Offices: Leading risk assessments & compliance
  • Federal Partners (PSP, CSIS, GAC): Providing threat intelligence
  • Cybersecurity Teams: Securing research data & infrastructure
  • Legal & Export Control Teams: Ensuring regulatory compliance

3. Tools & Best Practices

  • Standardized Risk Questionnaires (e.g., STRAC templates)
  • Automated Affiliation Checks (OpenSanctions, NRO lists)
  • Secure Collaboration Platforms for sensitive projects
  • Researcher Training on security best practices

Research Security Risk Assessment Portal: The Streamlit Web App

To operationalize research security assessments, we developed an interactive Streamlit web application that enables:

1. Automated Risk Assessments

  • Real-time threat scoring based on project details (funding sources, collaborators, research domain)
  • Standardized questionnaires aligned with Canadian security policies (NSGRP, STRAC)
  • Visual risk dashboards showing threat levels and mitigation recommendations

2. Collaboration Screening

  • Automated checks against Canadian sanctions lists and high-risk entities
  • Affiliation vetting for international partners
  • Export control flagging for sensitive technologies

3. Continuous Monitoring & Reporting

  • Dynamic risk registers tracking project security postures over time
  • Automated alerts for emerging threats (cyber risks, geopolitical changes)
  • Executive reporting for institutional leadership and federal compliance

The Way Forward for Canada

Research security is not about isolation—it's about smart protection. By adopting a risk-based, adaptable framework, Canadian institutions can:

  • Protect cutting-edge research from theft or misuse
  • Maintain trust with government and industry funders
  • Enable secure global collaborations with like-minded partners
  • Stay ahead of evolving threats in a competitive R&D landscape

As Canada invests in critical technologies, a strong research security framework ensures that innovation thrives—safely and securely.

Age Bias Detection and Mitigation Strategies for Credit Decision Making

View on GitHub

This project focuses on detecting and mitigating age-related bias in credit decision-making using machine learning models. Bias in automated credit scoring can lead to unfair decisions, especially regarding sensitive attributes like age. The goal is to assess and mitigate age bias in predictive models built using the German Credit dataset and improve the fairness of credit decisions across different age groups.

Problem Statement

  • In financial decision-making, such as loan approvals, biased models can negatively impact specific groups of individuals. This project aims to explore how age bias affects credit decision models and implement bias mitigation strategies to promote fairer outcomes. We analyze the disparity between the privileged group (age ≥ 25) and the unprivileged group (age < 25) in the model's decisions.

Methodology

1

Bias Detection

  • We begin by training machine learning models (Logistic Regression and Random Forest) on the dataset without applying any bias mitigation techniques.
  • Fairness metrics such as Disparate Impact, Average Odds Difference, Equal Opportunity Difference, and Statistical Parity Difference are computed to evaluate the presence of bias based on the protected attribute: Age.
2

Bias Mitigation (Reweighing)

  • After detecting bias, we apply the Reweighing technique (a pre-processing bias mitigation algorithm). This technique adjusts the weights of the training data to balance the outcomes for privileged and unprivileged groups.
  • We retrain the models using the transformed dataset and reassess bias using the same fairness metrics.
3

Model Evaluation

  • The models are evaluated both for accuracy and fairness. We compare the performance before and after bias mitigation to assess the effectiveness of the reweighing technique.
  • The fairness metrics are computed on both the original and transformed datasets to measure the change in bias.

Results

  • Without Bias Mitigation: Initial analysis shows that the privileged group (age ≥ 25) received more favorable credit outcomes compared to the unprivileged group (age < 25).
  • With Bias Mitigation (Reweighing): After applying the reweighing technique, there was a notable improvement in fairness metrics, such as Disparate Impact and Equal Opportunity Difference, while maintaining a comparable level of model accuracy.
2.10

Hardening a Deep Learning–Based Intrusion Detection System Against Adversarial Attacks

Executive Summary

Modern Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) increasingly rely on deep learning models to classify network traffic as benign or malicious. While these models achieve high accuracy on clean data, they are demonstrably vulnerable to adversarial attacks—carefully crafted perturbations that cause malicious traffic to be misclassified as benign. This project systematically evaluates this vulnerability and implements practical defense mechanisms to harden a deep learning–based IDS, with direct applicability to critical infrastructure environments.

The project follows a structured, end-to-end methodology consisting of four phases: build, attack, defend, and evaluate. First, a baseline IDS is developed using a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural network trained on the CIC-IDS2017 dataset, reformulated as a binary classification problem (benign vs. attack traffic). Rigorous preprocessing—including data cleaning, normalization, and reshaping for sequence modeling—ensures a robust experimental foundation. The baseline model achieves near-perfect accuracy on clean test data, establishing a strong reference point.

Second, the system's resilience is intentionally challenged using adversarial machine learning techniques. Leveraging the Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART), Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) attacks are generated under a white-box threat model. Results clearly demonstrate a severe degradation in detection capability—accuracy dropping from ~99% on clean data to nearly random performance under adversarial conditions—highlighting the fragility of standard deep learning–based IDS when faced with adaptive attackers.

Third, three complementary defense mechanisms are implemented and analyzed.

1

Adversarial Training

Augments the training set with adversarial examples, forcing the model to learn more robust decision boundaries.

2

Defensive Distillation

Applies a teacher–student learning paradigm with softened labels to smooth class boundaries and reduce sensitivity to small perturbations.

3

Feature Squeezing

Acts as a lightweight, inference-time defense by reducing input feature precision and collapsing adversarial noise.

In the final evaluation phase, all defended models are benchmarked against both clean and adversarial test data. Adversarial training emerges as the most effective defense, restoring adversarial accuracy to above 90% with minimal loss on clean data. Feature squeezing provides a strong low-cost mitigation suitable for real-time or edge deployments, while defensive distillation offers moderate resilience.

Real-World Use Case: Electric Vehicle (EV) Charging Networks

Beyond academic validation, the project is extended to a real-world use case: Electric Vehicle (EV) charging networks. By mapping IDS features and adversarial threat models to Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP) traffic, the work demonstrates how adversarially hardened IDS can protect against stealthy manipulation of billing, availability, and load-balancing messages—directly supporting smart grid stability and critical infrastructure security.

Strategic Value

This project delivers a practical blueprint for adversarially robust IDS design, combining empirical rigor with deployable defenses. It aligns with emerging research-security and critical-infrastructure protection priorities by explicitly addressing adversarial AI risks in cyber-physical and energy–mobility systems.
