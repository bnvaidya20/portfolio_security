Comprehensive Partner Risk Screening Tool Using Streamlit

View on GitHub

Introduction

Canada's research ecosystem is globally recognized for its excellence, openness, and collaborative spirit. However, this openness also introduces vulnerabilities, making it a potential target for foreign interference. The unauthorized transfer of knowledge — particularly in high-impact research areas such as artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and genetic engineering — can lead to the misuse of research for military or surveillance purposes. Such risks pose significant national security challenges for Canada and its international partners.

In January 2024, the Government of Canada introduced the Policy on Sensitive Technology Research and Affiliations of Concern (STRAC), which outlines mandatory requirements for federally funded research projects. The policy prohibits funding where any named researcher is affiliated with or receives support from organizations identified as national security risks.

This project explores a Comprehensive Partner Risk Screening Tool built with Streamlit that evaluates both organizational profiles and technology sensitivity to provide comprehensive risk assessments. This tool, implemented using Streamlit, allows research administrators and investigators to assess potential risks by referencing:

  • Sensitive Technology Research Areas (STRA)
  • Named Research Organizations (NRO)

It acts as a decision-support mechanism to assist institutions in maintaining compliance with the federal policy.

Key Policy Links

  • Policy on Sensitive Technology Research and Affiliations of Concern (STRAC)
  • Sensitive Technology Research Areas List, January 2024
  • Named Research Organizations List, January 2024

Compliance Logic Used by the Tool

This tool follows the compliance structure defined in the STRAC policy:

  • Determine if the research aims to advance any STRA-defined sensitive technologies.
  • Verify whether any researcher is currently affiliated with, or receives any support (financial or in-kind), from an NRO.

If both conditions are met, the research funding is likely to be ineligible.

Note: Past affiliations are not considered under this policy unless there is a continuing obligation or formal return agreement.

Risk Classification Scheme

Risk categories in this tool are used for internal assessment purposes ONLY and do not reflect any official risk scale issued by the Government of Canada. The tool having five risk levels employs the following logic:

CRITICAL

Match with STRA and affiliation to critical-risk or high-risk NRO.

HIGH

Match with STRA and affiliation to medium-risk NRO.

ELEVATED

No STRA match but affiliation with critical-risk or high-risk NRO.

MODERATE

No STRA match but affiliation to medium-risk NRO or No NRO affiliation but match with STRA.

LOW

No STRA match or NRO affiliation detected.

Scripts Breakdown

  • Load data using pandas.

  • Load and view metadata.
  • Manage data source information.

3. data_validation_gx.py

This Python script leverages Great Expectations to validate data based on defined expectations.

Key Features:

  • Initialization: Initializes a Great Expectations context and loads data.
  • Expectation Suite: Defines various validation checks (expectations) such as:
    • Ensuring non-null values in specific columns.
    • Checking value ranges for numeric columns.
    • Validating categorical values.
  • Checkpointing: Runs the validation against the dataset and provides a report with results.
  • Result Review: Detailed review of validation results, including why any checks failed.

Workflow

  1. Load Data: Load a CSV file into a pandas DataFrame.
  2. Initialize Great Expectations: Set up a Great Expectations context.
  3. Define Expectations: Create expectations to validate the data against certain rules.
  4. Run Checkpoints: Run a checkpoint to validate the dataset against the expectations suite.
  5. Review Results: Review the results to check if the dataset meets all expectations and address any validation failures.

Geospatial Analysis Using QGIS for Threat Assessment and Incident Response

The Geospatial Analysis Using QGIS project presents a comprehensive, end-to-end framework for implementing a Geospatial Threat Intelligence and Incident Response System. Built entirely on open-source technologies, it integrates data science, spatial analytics, and geovisualization to enable real-time situational awareness, strategic decision-making, and operational optimization for security-focused environments.

The project is organized into six structured modules, each contributing to the development of a fully operational geospatial intelligence platform.

Module 1: Foundation & Data Generation

Establishes the foundation by generating synthetic datasets—including threat actor locations, security incidents, critical infrastructure, and resource units—using Python with pandas and geopandas. These datasets, standardized to the WGS84 (EPSG:4326) coordinate system, provide realistic inputs for subsequent spatial analyses within QGIS.

Module 2: Data Ingestion & Layer Creation

Focuses on data ingestion and layer creation within QGIS. Each dataset is imported as a vector or delimited text layer, styled by attributes such as risk level or severity, and symbolized using categorical color schemes. Module 3: Temporal Visualization & Heatmaps

Advances the analysis through temporal visualization and heatmap generation. By leveraging the TimeManager and Heatmap plugins, the project enables temporal animation of incident data and kernel density estimation for identifying geographic hotspots. Optional clustering using DBSCAN enhances analytical depth by revealing hidden spatial patterns of threat concentration.

Module 4: Proximity & Optimization Analysis

Shifts focus to proximity and optimization analysis. Spatial buffers are generated around high-risk threat actors, and spatial joins identify critical infrastructures within these risk zones. Combined with resource location data, QGIS-based service area and gap analyses provide a foundation for strategic resource deployment, highlighting areas of under-coverage and priority risk zones.

Module 5: Report Automation & Dashboarding

Introduces report automation and dashboarding using QGIS Atlas and DataPlotly. Automated incident reports, interactive dashboards, and dynamic charts consolidate all spatial outputs into actionable intelligence products, supporting executive-level briefings and research security applications.

Module 6: System Architecture & Deployment

Documents the system architecture and deployment pipeline, illustrating a fully modular, scalable, and production-ready ecosystem. The final deliverables include Python-based data generation scripts, QGIS project files, heatmaps, proximity maps, and an architectural diagram, all designed for institutional, research, and operational use.

Project Impact

This project exemplifies how open-source geospatial technologies can operationalize threat intelligence—transforming static data into dynamic, interactive intelligence systems that enhance research security, executive protection, and incident response capabilities.

Secure Electric Vehicle Ecosystem for Smart Grid (SecCharge System)

Executive Overview

SecCharge: Secure Electric Vehicle Ecosystem for Smart Grid is a secure, multi-tier electric-mobility platform that enables end-to-end EV charging services—from user onboarding and reservations to authorization, charging session orchestration, metering, billing, and third-party settlement. The platform evolved through several iterations (2014–2017), with the Improved SecCharge System (2017) strengthening the architecture around a Spring-based Application Server, JWT-secured APIs, Angular single-page web client, Android mobile clients (including an embedded AEV client), and a Control Center (CC) implementing Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP) for wired and wireless charging stations (CS/WCS).

1. System Architecture (Priority: Improved SecCharge System)

1.1 High-Level Topology (2017)

The Improved SecCharge architecture organizes the platform into the following components, interacting primarily via REST over HTTP(S):

  • Web App (Angular SPA): Single-page application for participating users (search, reservation, charging control, trip planning) and service-provider functions (manage sites/stations). End-to-end testing leverages Protractor.
  • Android Mobile App: Parity with the web app for participating users.
  • AEV Android App: Embedded in the vehicle (on a Raspberry Pi for demo). Monitors the vehicle State-of-Charge (SoC) and proactively reserves a station when the SoC drops below a critical threshold; not distributed to end users.
  • Application Server (Spring Boot): The transactional heart of SecCharge. Exposes REST APIs to clients and backend services. Responsible for all database CRUD, enforces endpoint security, and applies JWT-based stateless authentication/authorization.
  • Authentication Server (EV Only): Validates EV contract certificates sent by CS via CC and App Server; only authenticated EVs can start charging.
  • Control Center (CC): Manages protocol exchanges with Charging Stations (CS) and Wireless Charging Stations (WCS) using OCPP; forwards telemetry such as SoC and executes remote start/stop transactions.
  • CS/WCS (Raspberry Pi): Field gateways executing port control, local authorization, and periodic telemetry. On EV connection, the station sends the EV authentication certificate (for EVs) and SoC readings.
  • Database Server: Durable storage for users, assets (provider/site/station/ports), reservations, prices, and session/transaction/billing artifacts.

1.2 Security Model

SecCharge's defense-in-depth is layered as follows:

  • Transport Security: HTTP(S) for client–server; HTTPS with SSL/TLS for third-party payment integrations.
  • API Security: Spring Security guards endpoints; per-endpoint authorization via ant-matchers; JWT (JSON Web Token) conveys authenticated identity, roles, and claims for stateless, horizontally scalable sessions.
  • Error Handling: Centralized @RestControllerAdvice-based exception handling provides consistent client feedback.
  • Credential Hygiene: BCrypt (adaptive, slow hashing) hardens password storage against brute-force attacks.
  • Identity & Certificates (EV): For EV-side identification, SecCharge adopts ISO/IEC 15118's V2G PKI model: EVs receive contract certificates from an issuing CA.

2. System Implementation (Priority: Improved SecCharge System)

2.1 Application Server (Spring Boot)

  • CORS Policies: Configured to permit approved origins for web/mobile front ends
  • Swagger/OpenAPI: Auto-documents REST endpoints
  • Endpoint Security: Custom HttpSecurity config secures sensitive routes
  • JWT Auth Flow: Stateless authentication with bearer tokens
  • Reservation APIs: Search CSSs within a radius with optional filters

2.2 Control Center (OCPP) and Station Integration

OCPP Operations implemented/benchmarked against Syntronic charging stations include Authorize, StartTransaction, StopTransaction, MeterValues, Heartbeat, and BootNotification.

2.3 Trip/Route Planning

Trip planning combines Google Directions alternatives with SoC-aware critical points detection.

3. Representative Use Cases

3.1 Mobile & Web

  • UC-1.1 Login: Role-based entry point with lockout after repeated failures
  • UC-1.2 Find a Charging Station: Triggered by low SoC or by user intent
  • Start/Cancel Charging: After reservation and pre-authorization
  • Trip Planning: Enter origin/destination, review recommended routes
  • Manage Payments: Add/edit/delete third-party accounts

3.2 Administrative

  • SecCharge Administrator (SA): Add/modify/delete user accounts; reset passwords; audit actions
  • Charging Station Administrator (CSA): Commission/decommission stations; update site/station metadata

3.3 EV/AEV Automations

  • AEV SoC-Driven Reservation: Embedded client monitors SoC and auto-reserves when below threshold
  • EV Plug-and-Charge: EV presents contract certificate; station/CC/App/Authentication Server coordinate certificate validation

AI-Driven EV Charging Management System

Introduction

The rapid adoption of electric vehicles (EVs) has created new challenges in managing charging infrastructure efficiently and sustainably. Traditional charging management systems often rely on static scheduling, limited user interaction, and manual oversight. To address these gaps, the AI-driven EV Charging Management System (EV-CMS) was designed and implemented.

It fuses predictive analytics, optimization, and IoT-enabled emulation to orchestrate reservations, pricing, and charger control with measurable gains in utilization, waiting time, and operating cost.

This system comprises three major components:

  1. Backend Infrastructure — Application and database servers responsible for reservations, scheduling, and optimization.
  2. Frontend Applications — Web and mobile apps that provide user-friendly interfaces for reservations, monitoring, and billing.
  3. Hardware Emulation Layer — Raspberry Pi-based EV Charging Station (EVCS) emulator, Jetson Nano for self-driving vehicle navigation, and static wireless charging emulation.

Core Features

Slot-Based Reservations

  • Supports a minimum of 15-minute slots for Level 3 (DC fast charging) and 30-minute slots for Level 2 (AC charging)
  • Users can reserve charging sessions in advance through the app
  • Enforces alignment to slot boundaries, configurable grace periods, and no-show handling

AI-Powered Optimization

  • Demand Forecasting: Uses time-series models and machine learning (XGBoost, LSTM) to predict charger demand
  • Scheduling: Applies mixed-integer programming (MIP/CP-SAT) to assign slots fairly and minimize waiting times
  • Dynamic Pricing: Reinforcement learning adjusts tariffs based on demand, occupancy, and grid constraints
  • Anomaly & Faults: Monitors charger telemetry for faults, abnormal energy consumption, and cyber anomalies

Hardware Integration

  • Raspberry Pi EVCS Emulator: Simulates charger behavior using OCPP-like communication
  • Self-Driving Demo: A scaled vehicle equipped with Jetson Nano, camera, ultrasonic sensors autonomously navigates to its reserved charging bay
  • Wireless Static Charging Emulation: Models charging efficiency based on pad alignment and vehicle positioning

System Architecture

Backend

  • API Gateway: REST/gRPC for apps and devices; JWT for apps, optional mTLS for devices
  • Scheduling Engine: Rolling-horizon optimizer with greedy warm-start
  • Data Layer: PostgreSQL + TimescaleDB for sessions & telemetry; Redis cache
  • AI Services: Forecasting, dynamic pricing, anomaly detection, and fault prediction

Frontend

  • Web App: Booking, status, and administration (React)
  • Mobile App: Reservations, alerts, navigation to bay, push notifications
  • Admin Console: Site power caps, tariffs, DR events, alerts triage

Hardware/Edge

  • Raspberry Pi: EVCS state machine; MeterValues, StatusNotification
  • Jetson Nano: Visual servoing for bay/pad alignment; ultrasonic for near-field safety
  • Sensors: Camera + ultrasonic; optional current/temperature for richer emulation

Demonstration & Use Cases

EV Owner

A driver reserves a multiple of 30-minute Level 2 slot, navigates to the station, and monitors charging progress in the app.

Self-Driving Vehicle Demo

A Jetson-powered miniature Autonomous Electric Vehicle (AEV) automatically drives to its assigned bay, aligns with a wireless charging pad, and begins charging.

Operator Dashboard

An admin monitors live charger occupancy, demand forecasts, and anomaly alerts, while adjusting site limits during a simulated demand-response event.

Adaptive Micro-Segmentation & Access Control for Power Grid OT Networks

Executive Summary

The convergence of Information Technology (IT) and Operational Technology (OT) in modern power grids has dramatically expanded the cyber-attack surface while preserving stringent 99.999% availability requirements. Traditional perimeter-based security models—static firewalls, VLAN zoning, and manual access controls—are fundamentally inadequate: they cannot adapt to dynamic grid states, treat intra-zone traffic as inherently trusted, require days for policy updates, and generate overwhelming alert volumes that paralyze security operators.

To address these limitations, AMSCOT (Adaptive Micro-Segmentation and Control for OT Networks) introduces a novel, AI-driven security framework engineered specifically for power grid environments. It shifts the paradigm from static perimeter defense to continuous, context-aware, per-device-pair micro-segmentation. At its core lies the Neuro-Fuzzy Integrated Security Engine (NFISE), a tri-modal AI architecture that synergizes fuzzy inference for operator-interpretable decisions, Deep Conv-LSTM networks for temporal pattern recognition in OT traffic, and self-supervised contrastive learning for unsupervised behavioral grounding and zero-day drift detection.

By eliminating dependency on labeled attack datasets while delivering both high accuracy and explainability, AMSCOT enables utilities to dynamically evaluate device criticality, enforce granular security boundaries, and execute proportional response actions. This approach ensures robust threat containment without compromising the ultra-high availability and deterministic latency requirements of critical grid operations.

1

Tri-Modal AI Engine (NFISE)

Integrates Fuzzy Inference for transparent decision-making, Deep Conv-LSTMs for temporal traffic analysis, and Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning to establish behavioral baselines and detect zero-day anomalies without requiring labeled attack datasets.

2

Dynamic Criticality & Micro-Segmentation

Continuously re-evaluates device importance based on real-time grid topology, load conditions, and threat context, automatically generating and adjusting per-device-pair security boundaries to prevent lateral movement within traditionally trusted zones.

3

N-Tier Graduated Response & Protocol Enforcement

Implements a proportional response continuum—from enhanced monitoring to emergency containment—coupled with native OT protocol awareness (DNP3, IEC 61850, Modbus) to guarantee zero added latency to protection-critical traffic.

Simulation and hardware-in-the-loop testing across 847 attack scenarios validate the framework's operational superiority, achieving a 97.3% True Positive Rate for known attacks and 89.1% for zero-day anomalies, with a remarkably low 2.1% False Positive Rate—a 15–30× improvement over traditional SIEMs. The system executes Tier 3–4 containment actions in under 50 milliseconds while maintaining zero packet loss during normal operations. Economically, AMSCOT delivers a 63.7% reduction in 5-year Total Cost of Ownership and cuts security operational costs by 40–60% through 97.9% alert pre-filtering and ~82% automated NERC CIP compliance evidence collection.

Real-World Use Case: Brownfield & Greenfield Utility Deployment

Beyond controlled simulations, AMSCOT is engineered for flexible real-world deployment across diverse utility infrastructures. Its passive TAP-based integration introduces zero latency to legacy brownfield environments, while SDN-native capabilities support modern greenfield deployments. This scalable architecture accommodates utilities of all sizes—from small municipal cooperatives to large transmission organizations—via tiered sizing and cooperative cost-sharing models.

Strategic Value

AMSCOT resolves the fundamental OT security paradox: maintaining ultra-high grid availability while enforcing intelligent, adaptive access control. By combining tri-modal AI fusion, proportional response mechanisms, and economically optimized architecture, it provides utilities with a scientifically validated, regulatorily aligned pathway to modernize cybersecurity postures—transitioning from reactive compliance to proactive, human-AI collaborative defense that safeguards critical power infrastructure against increasingly sophisticated threats.

AI and Quantum-Inspired Approaches for Enhancing Security in the Internet of Military Defense Things

The Internet of Military Defense Things (IoMDT) represents a transformative shift in modern defense operations, integrating heterogeneous platforms—including UAVs, UUVs, USVs, autonomous robots, and distributed sensor networks—into a unified, data-driven ecosystem. This interconnected architecture enhances situational awareness, enables autonomous decision-making, and supports coordinated multi-domain operations across land, air, sea, and cyber environments.

However, the same characteristics that make IoMDT operationally powerful—scale, heterogeneity, real-time data exchange, and deployment in hostile environments—also introduce significant security challenges. IoMDT systems are highly susceptible to cyberattacks, spoofing, data manipulation, stealthy intrusions, and anomalous operational behaviors that traditional rule-based or signature-driven security mechanisms cannot reliably detect.

This paper addresses these challenges by proposing advanced AI-driven and Quantum-Inspired Machine Learning (QIML) approaches to enhance real-time threat detection and security resilience in IoMDT networks. The work focuses on anomaly detection as a core defensive capability, recognizing that many modern military cyber and cyber-physical attacks manifest as subtle deviations from normal behavior rather than known attack signatures.

Key Contribution: Hybrid VGAE-IF Model

A key contribution is the proposed Hybrid Variational Graph Autoencoder with Isolation Forest (Hybrid VGAE-IF) model. By modeling IoMDT systems as graphs—where nodes represent devices and edges represent communication or interaction patterns—the approach captures relational intelligence that is often missed by conventional anomaly detection methods.

The Variational Graph Autoencoder learns latent representations of normal network behavior, while the Isolation Forest component isolates statistical outliers in the learned embedding space. This hybrid design allows the system to detect both structural anomalies (e.g., abnormal communication patterns or coordination failures) and feature-level anomalies (e.g., unexpected sensor readings).

Quantum-Inspired Machine Learning (QIML)

In parallel, the paper explores Quantum-Inspired Machine Learning (QIML) techniques—including Quantum-Inspired Support Vector Machines (QSVM), Quantum-Inspired Random Forests (QRF), and Quantum Neural Networks (QNN)—to address the challenges of high-dimensional, large-scale IoMDT data. These methods leverage quantum-inspired feature mappings and kernel transformations to improve pattern separation and learning efficiency without requiring quantum hardware.

Overall Impact

Overall, the paper demonstrates that combining graph-based deep learning with quantum-inspired models provides a robust, scalable, and forward-looking security framework for IoMDT. These approaches enable adaptive, unsupervised, and real-time threat detection, significantly enhancing the resilience, autonomy, and operational reliability of military IoT systems in contested environments.

Optimizing Bikesharing with Quantum-Inspired Techniques: Demand Prediction and Fleet Rebalancing

This work presents a comprehensive, quantum-inspired optimization framework aimed at improving the operational efficiency of urban bikesharing systems through enhanced demand prediction and optimized fleet rebalancing. Bikesharing has become a critical component of sustainable urban mobility, yet operators consistently face challenges such as spatial–temporal demand imbalance, bike shortages at high-demand stations, and surplus inventory at low-demand locations. Traditional forecasting and optimization approaches often struggle with the nonlinear, dynamic, and data-intensive nature of these systems.

Quantum-Inspired Approach

To address these challenges, the study introduces quantum-inspired machine learning and optimization techniques, which adapt principles from quantum mechanics—such as superposition, tunneling, and high-dimensional state representation—for use on classical computing platforms. The objective is twofold: accurately predict bikesharing demand and compute efficient bike redistribution strategies that minimize imbalance and operational cost while improving user satisfaction.

Demand Prediction with QINN

For demand prediction, the framework employs a Quantum-Inspired Neural Network (QINN). Unlike conventional neural networks, the QINN integrates adaptive learning rates influenced by quantum-inspired gradient dynamics, enabling the model to escape local minima more effectively.

Fleet Rebalancing Optimization

For fleet rebalancing, the study formulates bike redistribution as an optimization problem that minimizes the imbalance between predicted demand and actual bike availability across stations. Quantum-inspired optimization methods—such as simulated annealing-like dynamics and gradient-based solvers—are applied to identify efficient redistribution strategies under realistic constraints.

Implementation & Evaluation

The framework is implemented and evaluated using the SF Bay Area Bikeshare dataset, which integrates trip records, station metadata, and weather information. Empirical results demonstrate that quantum-inspired demand prediction achieves higher forecasting accuracy than traditional baselines, while the rebalancing optimization substantially reduces supply–demand mismatch.

Conclusion

Overall, this study demonstrates that quantum-inspired techniques offer a powerful and scalable alternative for managing complex urban mobility systems. While acknowledging computational complexity and assumptions of stationarity as limitations, the work highlights strong potential for extension to real-time IoT-enabled bikesharing systems and other shared mobility services. It establishes a forward-looking foundation for applying quantum-inspired optimization to large-scale, data-driven transportation challenges.
