Threat Intelligence Platform & Real-Time Dashboard for Healthcare

View on GitHub

Why Healthcare Needs Threat Intelligence

In today's digital age, healthcare organizations are among the most targeted sectors by cybercriminals. The sensitivity of Protected Health Information (PHI), the adoption of connected medical devices, and the increasing complexity of IT systems make healthcare a prime target for cyber threats.

To tackle this challenge, we designed a Threat Intelligence Platform (TIP) paired with a Real-Time Dashboard—a unified system that empowers security teams to proactively detect, analyze, and respond to threats targeting healthcare systems.

Project Overview

The platform is a modular, open-source solution specifically built to serve the needs of healthcare institutions. It aggregates global cyber threat data, enriches it with context, maps it to adversarial behaviors, and visualizes emerging risks in real time—all in one place.

Key Features

Automated Threat Feed Ingestion

Continuously pulls open-source intelligence (OSINT) from global threat databases such as CIRCL and Botvrij.eu.

Context-Aware Threat Enrichment

Integrates with services like VirusTotal and GreyNoise to enrich indicators of compromise (IOCs) with real-world context.

MITRE ATT&CK Mapping

Links threat patterns to known adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures for meaningful behavior analysis.

Risk-Based Scoring Engine

Computes risk scores based on severity levels (e.g., CVSS) and proximity to PHI infrastructure.

Real-Time Visualization

Presents threat insights through a live dashboard tailored for healthcare threat analysts and executives.

Workflow at a Glance

  1. MISP Setup: A dedicated MISP instance is deployed using Docker, configured to pull open threat feeds such as CIRCL and Botvrij.eu. And extract healthcare-related events from these MISP feeds.
  2. Connection Initialization: The platform establishes a secure link with MISP to fetch recent threat events and associated attributes.
  3. Local Data Storage: A lightweight SQLite database is initialized to store, normalize, and correlate threat data.
  4. Data Ingestion: The ingestion pipeline collects structured (STIX) and unstructured data, including HIPAA breach disclosures.
  5. Threat Enrichment: Each indicator is contextually enriched using open APIs like VirusTotal (file/malware reputation) and GreyNoise (noise vs. targeted scans).
  6. TTP Mapping & Scoring: Indicators are mapped to MITRE ATT&CK tactics and scored based on severity and potential PHI impact.
  7. Real-Time Threat Monitoring: A user-friendly Streamlit dashboard presents a live view of current threats, high-risk IOCs, and incident trends.

Context-Aware Behavioral SOAR System

Context-Aware Behavioral SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation & Response) System is a next-generation, risk-adaptive orchestration platform designed to transform security operations by reducing noise, improving analyst efficiency, and ensuring complete auditability of incident response. It integrates detection, enrichment, risk scoring, automation, and validation into a closed-loop response fabric tailored for modern threat environments.

Core Concept

The system ingests Wazuh alerts and enriches them with both external threat intelligence (VirusTotal, AbuseIPDB, MISP, Shodan, GreyNoise) and internal business context (asset criticality, identity risk, and anomaly scores). A composite risk score (0.4·Threat Intel + 0.4·Asset Criticality + 0.2·Anomaly) determines whether an event is promoted into PostgreSQL-based incident storage and visualized in Grafana, suppressing noise while focusing on high-value threats.

Key Capabilities

Context-aware triage

Risk-driven event promotion using dynamic thresholds to adapt during alert storms.

Bi-directional automation

Firewall blocks, Okta/Entra session revocations, and DB freezes, followed by validation with Loki, Wazuh, and Nmap.

Proactive threat hunting

Daily IoC enrichment and hunting boards surfaced via dashboards.

Full traceability

Every enrichment, decision, and action logged in PostgreSQL and mirrored to Loki/Wazuh.

Operational visibility

Grafana dashboards covering incident flow, KPIs (MTTD/MTTR), hunting status, and system health.

Deployment & Architecture

The system supports both single-host PoC deployments and hardened two-VM production setups:

  • VM-A: Wazuh, Loki, Promtail (detection and log management).
  • VM-B: Shuffle SOAR, PostgreSQL, Grafana, with optional Nmap/YARA.
    Collaboration occurs through Slack interactive approvals or an optional analyst portal, ensuring secure, role-based engagement.

Strategic Value

  • Efficiency gains: Cuts promoted alert volume by 30%+ and improves detection/response times by at least 25%.
  • Governance & compliance: End-to-end traceability, GDPR/DPO notifications, and strict least-privilege design.
  • Scalability: Modular deployments from PoC to production with dynamic policy-driven risk tuning.

Success Metrics

  • ≥30% reduction in alert noise week-over-week.
  • ≥25% improvement in median MTTD/MTTR.
  • ≥95% of automated actions validated within 15 minutes.
  • <2% enrichment/API error rate per 24 hours.

Conclusion

The Context-Aware Behavioral SOAR System delivers a high-fidelity, low-noise, and fully auditable incident response fabric that bridges automation, behavioral context, and analyst oversight. By combining proactive hunting, closed-loop validation, and compliance-centric auditing, it equips organizations with a future-ready security operations platform that scales seamlessly across environments.

Age Bias Detection and Mitigation Strategies for Credit Decision Making

View on GitHub

This project focuses on detecting and mitigating age-related bias in credit decision-making using machine learning models. Bias in automated credit scoring can lead to unfair decisions, especially regarding sensitive attributes like age. The goal is to assess and mitigate age bias in predictive models built using the German Credit dataset and improve the fairness of credit decisions across different age groups.

Problem Statement

In financial decision-making, such as loan approvals, biased models can negatively impact specific groups of individuals. This project aims to explore how age bias affects credit decision models and implement bias mitigation strategies to promote fairer outcomes. We analyze the disparity between the privileged group (age ≥ 25) and the unprivileged group (age < 25) in the model's decisions.

Methodology

1. Bias Detection

  • We begin by training machine learning models (Logistic Regression and Random Forest) on the dataset without applying any bias mitigation techniques.
  • Fairness metrics such as Disparate Impact, Average Odds Difference, Equal Opportunity Difference, and Statistical Parity Difference are computed to evaluate the presence of bias based on the protected attribute: Age.

2. Bias Mitigation (Reweighing)

  • After detecting bias, we apply the Reweighing technique (a pre-processing bias mitigation algorithm). This technique adjusts the weights of the training data to balance the outcomes for privileged and unprivileged groups.
  • We retrain the models using the transformed dataset and reassess bias using the same fairness metrics.

3. Model Evaluation

  • The models are evaluated both for accuracy and fairness. We compare the performance before and after bias mitigation to assess the effectiveness of the reweighing technique.
  • The fairness metrics are computed on both the original and transformed datasets to measure the change in bias.

Results

  • Without Bias Mitigation: Initial analysis shows that the privileged group (age ≥ 25) received more favorable credit outcomes compared to the unprivileged group (age < 25).
  • With Bias Mitigation (Reweighing): After applying the reweighing technique, there was a notable improvement in fairness metrics, such as Disparate Impact and Equal Opportunity Difference, while maintaining a comparable level of model accuracy.

GRC Tools Selection Criteria and Recommendations

Overview

This guide provides tailored recommendations and selection criteria to help organizations identify the most suitable open-source GRC tools based on their size, compliance requirements, and security focus.

Selection Criteria

1. Organization Size

  • Small to Medium Enterprises (SMEs): Tools like SimpleRisk and OpenGRC offer lightweight and cost-effective solutions for smaller teams with limited budgets.
  • Large Enterprises: Wazuh, Eramba, and OpenRMF provide robust scalability and advanced features for larger organizations.

2. Compliance Needs

  • Specific Frameworks: For ISO 27001, PCI DSS, or GDPR compliance, Eramba, GovReady-Q, and OpenRMF are excellent choices.
  • Broad Compliance: Tools like GraphGRC and Wazuh support multiple frameworks and provide templates for cross-framework compliance.

3. Security Focus

  • Incident Response: TheHive, GRR, and SOC-OpenSource specialize in incident tracking, response, and forensic data collection.
  • System Hardening: CalCom Hardening Suite automates system hardening and compliance checks.
  • SIEM Integration: OSSEC and Wazuh provide robust SIEM capabilities alongside compliance monitoring.

Recommendations

1. Broad GRC Needs

Recommended Tools: Eramba, GraphGRC, and GovReady-Q.

Why? These tools provide extensive compliance frameworks, policy management, and risk tracking, catering to organizations needing comprehensive governance, risk, and compliance management.

2. Security-Centric GRC

Recommended Tools: TheHive, GRR, OSSEC, and Wazuh.

Why? These tools focus on real-time security monitoring, incident response, and forensic analysis, making them ideal for security-heavy organizations.

3. NIST Compliance

Recommended Tools: OpenRMF and CalCom Hardening Suite.

Why? Both tools are specifically designed for managing NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF) compliance and related standards, providing automated workflows and checklists.

4. Compliance-First Approach

Recommended Tools: Eramba, GovReady-Q, and OpenGRC.

Why? These tools emphasize policy management, compliance templates, and audit tracking for organizations prioritizing compliance readiness.

5. Developer/DevSecOps Teams

Recommended Tools: OWASP Security Knowledge Framework (SKF) and GraphGRC.

Why? These tools provide developers with secure coding guidelines and frameworks, aligning security practices with development processes.

6. SOC Operations

Recommended Tools: SOC-OpenSource and Wazuh.

Why? These tools integrate modular solutions for threat intelligence, incident response, and log analysis, creating a comprehensive SOC environment.

7. Lightweight GRC Tools

Recommended Tools: SimpleRisk and OpenGRC.

Why? These tools are ideal for smaller teams and organizations needing straightforward risk and compliance management without heavy resource requirements.

OSINT-Driven Attribution & Darknet Threat Actor Profiling Pipeline

This project presents the design and phased implementation of an advanced, end-to-end Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT)—driven threat attribution pipeline that enables high-confidence identification, profiling, and tracking of cyber threat actors operating across the clear web, deep web, and darknet. The system moves beyond traditional IOC-centric and TTP-only approaches by integrating behavioral, linguistic, economic, and infrastructural signals into a unified intelligence model. Its strategic objective is to deliver context-rich, attribution-grade intelligence that answers not only what happened, but who is behind an activity, why they are acting, and how they are likely to evolve.

At its core, the pipeline is architected as a modular five-stage system: data collection, processing and enrichment, analysis and correlation, profiling and attribution, and dissemination. It begins with large-scale, non-attributable collection of data from darknet markets, underground forums, ransomware leak sites, social media, paste sites, code repositories, and technical intelligence feeds such as malware sandboxes and threat-intelligence platforms. All raw content is preserved for evidentiary integrity, ensuring traceability and analyst validation.

Collected data is then normalized, enriched, and transformed into structured intelligence. Advanced parsing and natural-language processing extract key entities such as aliases, PGP keys, cryptocurrency wallets, malware hashes, infrastructure artifacts, and linguistic characteristics. Dedicated enrichment services add contextual intelligence (WHOIS, passive DNS, blockchain analytics, malware analysis, and cross-platform alias discovery), converting fragmented artifacts into analytically valuable objects.

The analytical core of the system is a graph-based correlation engine, where actors, infrastructure, tools, and behaviors are modeled as interconnected entities. This enables discovery of hidden relationships—shared aliases, reused PGP keys, overlapping infrastructure, or recurring behavioral patterns—that are not visible through linear analysis. Automated malware family clustering using fuzzy hashing, YARA rules, and machine-learning–based similarity analysis further strengthens attribution confidence by linking campaigns and toolchains to specific actors or groups.

From this correlated intelligence, the pipeline synthesizes high-fidelity threat actor profiles. Each profile combines behavioral fingerprinting (language use, posting cadence, time-zone inference, and operational security habits) with observed TTPs mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework. Outputs include confidence-scored analytic judgements, associated infrastructure and financial artifacts, and visual representations aligned with the Diamond Model, supporting both strategic and operational use cases.

Crucially, the pipeline is designed for operational integration. Intelligence outputs are automatically transformed into STIX 2.1 objects and pushed to platforms such as MISP and OpenCTI, ensuring seamless consumption by SOC, threat-hunting, and incident-response teams. Automated reporting capabilities further enable executive-level briefings and cross-organizational intelligence sharing.

Strategic Value

This project delivers a scalable, legally mindful, and ethically governed attribution framework that bridges OSINT, malware analysis, and behavioral analytics. It enhances national-security and enterprise-defense postures by transforming disparate open-source signals into actionable, attribution-grade cyber threat intelligence—supporting proactive detection, informed response, and long-term adversary understanding.
